Silkroad Online Origin Mobile

Designing Without a Blueprint. — Rebuild a beloved 2004 PC MMORPG for mobile with no design docs, no formula, and a team of ≤ 3 designers.
Context

Silkroad Online (2004, Joymax) was a cultural reference point for an entire generation of Southeast Asian gamers. The task: faithfully rebuild it for mobile. The conditions made it far from a standard port — no design docs, no formula, proprietary engine, and 20 years of community reverse-engineering that still hadn't fully cracked the original combat formula.

The Real Challenge
Surface Problem

Rebuild a beloved game for mobile.

Port systems, adapt UX for touch, preserve the original. Straightforward — if you have the original designer's reasoning.

Actual Problem

Decode intent from 20-year-old decisions.

No author to ask. Understanding why those systems were built that way — and whether that reasoning still held — was the real challenge.

Reverse Engineering the Formula
1
Collected all community attempts — aggregated every formula variant from forums, wikis, and spreadsheets spanning 20 years. Mapped where each was accurate and where it broke.
2
Playtested and recorded all variables — controlled testing across multiple character builds. Built the raw empirical dataset no community attempt had done systematically.
3
Ran induction and interpolation loops — treated data as a convergence problem. Iterate until the formula held across the full stat range.
4
Honest disclosure to team: ~90% accuracy at launch. Disclosed uncertainty before launch — not hidden. Real player data refined it to 100% within 2-3 months live.
Economy Design Philosophy

In a free-trade MMORPG, the economy is the core loop. Central question: "What do I get for 1 hour of grinding?" — with the answer changing across the player journey:

EARLY · Hook Phase
High reward density → fast progression feel. Establish the loop before investment.
MID · Investment Phase
Rewards become meaningful. Drop density decreases, perceived value increases.
LATE · Ecosystem Phase
Rare items as goals → trading activity → living market economy.
What Broke (Transparent Failures)
⚔️ Combat Edge Cases
Rare stat combos fell outside formula range post-launch. Fix → Surgical per-case fixes, not full formula rebuild.
🤖 Bot Farm Monetization Exploit
Bot farms recovered 180% of invested currency in 30 days. Economic model assumed human behavior. Fix → Split currencies, rebalanced return ratios.
📉 Rare Item Inflation
Even 0.01% drop rate becomes a supply problem at bot-scale volume. Fix → Structural supply controls for non-human volume.

Yulgang Private Server — Hiệp Khách Giang Hồ

Reshape the culture. Rewrite the game. — A community-run MMORPG server I took from zero to 780 CCU through design innovation.
Background

Yulgang (MGame / VTC Vietnam) private server community. I entered as a player in 2017, moved through QA/QC into partner role, and ultimately took full ownership of game design and product decisions.

2017 · Player & Observer 2017 · QA/QC 2018 · Partner — Events & Ops 2018 · Game Designer + PO
The Challenge

⚠️ A Scene Frozen in Convention

Every private server followed the same formula — same systems, same events, same player loops. Innovation was absent. Rapid churn, bored community.

🧪 Design as Laboratory

Two principles: challenge outdated mindsets, treat each iteration as an A/B test. Willing to fail publicly before finding what worked.

Two Phases of Learning
PHASE 01 — BURN-THROUGH SERVER

Recalibrating ratios, stats, and values for a more engaging experience. Incremental. Precise. Player-tested.

📖 Learned: How to read what players need — not just what they say they want.
PHASE 02 — EXPERIMENTAL SERVER

Radical experimentation — modifying core to embed ideas from Phong Thần, Võ Lâm, Kiếm Thế, MU, and Laplace M.

📖 Learned: How to create experiences that didn't exist yet — and get a community to follow.
Three Key Design Decisions

01 / Circulatory Quest System

Signature Design

Most servers disabled the quest system entirely. When a developer accidentally left it active, I saw not a bug, but a structure — cyclical, repeatable loops that create daily habits. I refocused rewards: removed XP, added power-enhancing buffs with long-term value.

This is game design: not inventing from scratch, but seeing what's already there and understanding it better than anyone else.

02 / Dungeon Resource Distribution

Industry Influence

Killed auto-bot culture by tightening open-world resources and redistributing into dungeons — requiring active participation. Extra entries became a monetization lever. Community interaction increased as dungeons became shared content.

📊 From 2023, ~80% of Vietnamese private servers adopted this dungeon-based model. This server set the pattern.

03 / Celestial-Demonic Weapons

Ahead of Official

Designed a weapon tier requiring level 160 — a hard wall forcing full progression before unlocking special material farming. The ultimate weapon became a community status symbol and long-term aspiration.

🏆 In 2021 — three years later — Yulgang Official introduced a similar level-160 system. The private server designed ahead of the official game.

Supporting Case Study Live Ops Design GihOt Studio · 2022–2023

Dark Summoner — Live System Optimization

Optimize the live system. Build the structure that was missing.

Problems Inherited

No centralized metrics framework. Reward inflation from unstructured legacy events. Undocumented hero balance logic making new content risky.

What I Did

Built a metrics framework for the GD team. Restructured the live event model (cut reward inflation, consistent player expectation). Reverse-engineered and documented the balance formula for stable future updates.

Why It Mattered

Events became repeatable without escalation. New hero releases became predictable. The GD team gained a structured decision-making framework they didn't have before.

Key lesson: Read before writing. When inheriting a live product, the first task is understanding the previous designer's intent — even without explanation. Structure is a feature. Constraints clarify priorities.
