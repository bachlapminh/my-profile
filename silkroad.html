01 · Context

A 2004 PC MMORPG
that defined a generation.

Silkroad Online was released in 2004. It didn't just capture a playerbase — it became a cultural reference point for an entire generation of Southeast Asian gamers who grew up grinding its routes and trading its economy. It is still remembered 20 years later.

The task was to rebuild it for mobile. But the conditions made it far from a standard port.

No design document. No formula. No blueprint left behind by the original creator.
Built on an internal proprietary engine — no off-the-shelf tooling or precedent to reference.
Game Design team: never more than 3 people at any point across 21 months.
20 years of private server communities — thousands of hours of collective reverse-engineering — and still, no one had cracked the original combat formula completely.
02
02 · The Real Challenge

Not "what did they build?"
But "why did they build it that way?"

Surface Problem

Rebuild a beloved game for mobile.

This is the version everyone sees on the brief. Port the systems, adapt the UX for touch, preserve what made the original work. Straightforward enough — if you have the original designer's reasoning.

Actual Problem

Decode intent from 20-year-old decisions, with no author to ask.

The original creator left no explanation. Only the result. Understanding why those systems were built the way they were — and whether that reasoning still held after two decades — was the real design challenge.

The Core Tensions
→

20 years of private server communities collectively tried to reverse-engineer the original combat formula. None succeeded completely. The gaps were always there. This was the baseline I inherited.

→

After 20 years, was the original design still correct? Context changes. Player expectations change. What made sense for a 2004 PC playerbase may not apply to a 2022 mobile market in Southeast Asia.

→

The hardest judgment call: where to follow the original creator's logic faithfully, and where to apply independent judgment where the original may have been wrong — or where time had made it wrong.

🧭
The Transferable Skill This Demonstrates

Working with ambiguous direction and finding the real objective underneath the surface ask. When a boss gives vague instruction, when a document is missing, when the original decision-maker is unavailable — the ability to reconstruct intent from outcomes is the skill. This is what I was practicing for 21 months.

03 · Reverse Engineering the Formula

20 days of induction.
Converging on what no one had solved.

Community formulas existed. Players had spent years trying to crack the combat math. All of them were partially correct — accurate within certain stat ranges, wrong beyond them. No single formula held across the full progression curve.

01

Collect all community attempts

Aggregate every formula variant from forums, private server wikis, and player spreadsheets spanning 20 years. Map where each was accurate and where it broke — the failure points are the data.

02

Playtest and record all variables

Controlled testing: ATK, DEF, every relevant stat variable across multiple character builds. Record actual outcomes. Build the raw empirical dataset that no community attempt had done systematically.

03

Run induction and interpolation loops

Treat the data as a convergence problem. Iterate: propose formula → test against recorded outcomes → identify deviation → refine → repeat until the formula holds across the full stat range.

04

Honest disclosure to team: ~90% accuracy at launch

Higher level = higher stat values = larger absolute deviation. The formula held cleanly at low and mid ranges, but high-level content introduced larger margins. This was disclosed to the team before launch — not buried.

Why the 90% confession is a strength: Shipping with known, documented uncertainty is how good engineers work on live products. Pretending 100% confidence and then patching quietly is how trust collapses. Iterative methodology, not perfection, was the standard.

04 · Designing the Economy

Drop rate is not a number.
It's a philosophy.

In a free-trade MMORPG, the economy isn't a feature — it is the core loop. Every item drop, every rarity, every trading decision feeds back into why players return. The central design question had one sentence: "What do I get for 1 hour of grinding?"

The answer had to change depending on where the player was in their journey.

Early
Hook Phase

High reward density → fast progression feel. New players need to feel momentum immediately. Frequent drops, visible level gains, early equipment upgrades — the goal is to establish the grinding loop as satisfying before the player has any investment in the world.

Mid
Investment Phase

Rewards become meaningful → longer session investment. The player now has context. An item matters because they understand what it enables. Drop density decreases, but perceived value increases. Sessions lengthen naturally because goals feel achievable but require real effort.

Late
Ecosystem Phase

Rare items as long-term goals → trading activity → living ecosystem. Late game players aren't just grinding for themselves — they're participating in an economy. Item scarcity drives pricing, pricing drives trade, trade drives social interaction. The drop rate here isn't just a reward design decision; it's a market supply decision.

The result: grinding felt purposeful across all phases. Players stayed — and the retention numbers reflect it.

05 · What Broke

Three failures.
Each honest, each documented.

A live MMORPG with a free trade economy and an undocumented baseline is not a controlled experiment. Things broke. Here's what happened and how it was addressed — not the sanitized version.

⚔️ Combat Edge Cases

Rare statistical combinations — high DEF builds paired with certain elemental resistance stacking — produced damage outcomes that fell outside the formula's predicted range post-launch. The formula was correct for expected play patterns; it hadn't been stress-tested against every theoretical edge case at extreme stat values.

FIX →

Addressed per edge case rather than rebuilding the formula. A formula rebuild on a live game creates a cascade of secondary balance issues. Surgical fixes, properly documented, carried lower overall risk.

🤖 Monetization Exploit — Bot Farms

An investment package — pay upfront, receive currency returns per player level-up — was designed around expected organic leveling speed. What was not anticipated: bot farms operating at scale, leveling at speeds far beyond human players. After 30 days, bot accounts had recovered 180% of invested currency versus the actual revenue those packages should have generated. The market destabilized.

Leveling speed and leveling difficulty had both been underestimated for non-human operators. The economic model assumed human behavior.

FIX →

Rebalanced return ratios to reflect realistic operator scaling. Split the hard currency into a separate special currency for discount investment packages — removing cross-economy trading and isolating the exploit vector from the broader market.

📉 Rare Item Inflation

In a free-trade game, rare items function as publisher currency. Their scarcity is the mechanism that gives them value — and that value supports the entire in-game economy. But natural drop rates, even when deliberately low, cannot account for uncontrollable bot-volume multiplication. When bot farms run 24/7, even a 0.01% drop rate becomes a supply problem at scale.

FIX →

Redesigned the economic loop to ensure long-term market sustainability — introducing structural supply controls that accounted for non-human volume rather than assuming purely organic player activity.

07 · Lesson

What the rebuild
actually taught me.

"Rebuilding a 20-year-old game isn't about copying its features. It's about understanding why those features made people choose it over everything else — and whether that reason still holds today."

"The hardest design question isn't 'what should I build?' It's 'what was the original designer actually trying to solve — and was he right?'"

💡
The Real Skill — And Why It Transfers

Most design work comes with ambiguous direction. Briefs are incomplete. Managers give you context but not intent. The original author is unavailable. This project taught me to work backward from outcomes — to reconstruct the logic behind decisions that were never documented, and to decide when that logic still applied and when it didn't. That's not a game design skill. That's a career skill.
